About

As soon as I was old enough to hold a pencil, my father would inspire me to draw. Every day a different object; his hands guided me to blend colors and manipulate light and shadow.

A self-taught Photoshop ace by high school, I joined online forums to take upon avatar and signature design requests. I excessively applied filters and added swirly or grungy "dirty" brushes. Then, I learned how to code in HTML and created many websites on Angelfire with colorful backgrounds, lavishly beveled buttons, and my beloved marquee text.

Chico State University led me to pursue a career in Communication Design. I worked with InDesign, Illustrator, typography, and photography, eventually taking on a job at the school's newspaper as an ad designer. I won two awards during my employment, one for MVP and another for best ad design.

After college, I spent a little time freelancing and then joined a small web design company called Stark Marketing in San Jose. I worked side-by-side with a front-end engineer and learned how to properly code in HTML and CSS. We dealt with many clients and learned a bit about SEO.

My efforts took me to a design internship at Redbeacon, where I stil thrive today as a UI/UX Designer, and have since been acquired by The Home Depot. My marketing and advertising background proved useful, but I found my passion in user experience and interface, most notably in the Home Depot Pro shopping responsive website. During my time here I've also been fortunate enough to join design conferences like Warm Gun 2012 and Edward Tufte's One Day Course, work in a fast-paced environment with many cross-functional teams, and experience more growth day after day.

At home, I have a gray pooch named Sir Flops-A-Lot, whom my fiancé and I rescued in the summer of 2014. He loves lugging his toys around and playing fetch. My love for animals inspired the both of us to create a side project called Crittergo, which is a resource for first-time pet owners, preparing them with the right supplies to make their new critter's transition into their home a smooth one.

My other hobbies include yoga, singing, and crafting where I sell through a couple online shops for soap making and sewing baby shoes.

Pro Shopping

A big portion of Home Depot's sales are derived from home service professionals, called "pros". Pros include plumbers, painters, handymen, general contractors, house cleaners, and more. After extensive research, we found that pros thought of Home Depot as a corner store instead of their main shopping location.

We revamped the pro site and focused mainly on sales for pros. They were also unaware of the buy-online, pick-up-in-store service so we surfaced the feature upfront.

We were also mindful of the pros who are not first time users, creating a dashboard for their most frequent purchased items for re-purchasing and recommended items based on their purchases.

Everything is built on a one-page web application, creating a fast and smooth experience. The site is also responsive for tablet and mobile users while pushing smartphone users to download the free app.

Service Connect

Store employees, or "associates", are often asked by customers if Home Depot employs any professionals of home services. Combining Home Depot services and Redbeacon services, we created a tool to connect in-store customers with pros, called "Service Connect". This web app is accessible by any associate in the store while the customer is still with them for any detailed questions.

We featured the top ten most requested occupation at the top of the page for higher visibility, using images (as opposed to text links). If the occupation is not listed, the associate can use buttons below the images to browse.

We had a search bar concept, but it was out of scope. The search bar would make it easier to find the service because the associate can type in a keyword to search available services.

Redbeacon Quote/Message Platform

After a Redbeacon customer requests a service, pros in the area will be notified. A maximum of four pros are allowed to bid on a job. The customer then chooses a pro to complete the task.

We created special platforms for both customers and pros so Redbeacon can track the progress of the job and charge the pro when the job is finished. We installed a points system so the more the pro shops at Home Depot, the more jobs they can get.

I worked on the web version of these platforms in a team of two other designers. We worked closely with engineers to make sure the product looks good and functions well.

Price Estimator

Utilizing data from years of job requests, one of Redbeacon's engineers created a tool to estimate prices for future jobs. The algorithm was able to predict a price range depending on the ZIP code and job title.

I created the flow and interface for Price Estimator, using an existing graph design, and made sure the user is able to request a service without submitting another whole request.

The splash page of Price Estimator constantly cycles through images of handyman, floor installation, and electrician, while surfacing the estimates for those jobs. It also serves as a starting point for submitting an estimated price search.

The user is then asked questions pertaining to the request, such as number of rooms for house cleaning and basin material for sink repair.

When done, other information like location, scheduling, and other details are submitted through as a service request.

Home Experts

Experts is a Pinterest-esque project to capture more customers by allowing them to ask a DIY question to a home service professional. By creating a consumable free product, Redbeacon creates a healthier presence and gains customers' trust.

My responsibilities in this project includes giving UI advice, image stretching and manipulation, and creating icons.

Arby the Home Assistant

Arby is a stickman assistant for the app owner's home. The concept of the app was that the more the user inputs information, the "smarter" he becomes. Arby never became an app, but we were able to create a jquery mobile version.

My responsibilities included creating a user interface guideline, creating high-fidelity screens for each page, and slicing assets such as buttons, icons, and badges.
